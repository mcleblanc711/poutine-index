The Poutine Index

What does a poutine cost across Canada?

Map Methodology GitHub

Overview

The Poutine Index is inspired by The Economist's Big Mac Index, which uses the price of a Big Mac to compare purchasing power across countries. Similarly, we use classic poutine‚Äîa quintessentially Canadian dish‚Äîto compare food costs and affordability across Canadian cities.

What Qualifies as Classic Poutine?

For consistency, we only track prices for classic poutine, defined as:

  • French fries
  • Fresh cheese curds
  • Brown gravy

We exclude "loaded" or topped poutines (e.g., with pulled pork, bacon, vegetables, or other additions) because their prices vary significantly based on toppings, making comparisons unreliable.

Size Normalization

Restaurants use inconsistent size names (e.g., "regular" vs. "medium" vs. "single"). We normalize all sizes into three categories:

  • Small: The smallest portion offered, typically 200-300g
  • Regular: The standard/medium portion, or the only size if just one is offered
  • Large: The largest portion, sometimes called "family" or "jumbo"

When only one size is available, it is classified as "Regular" for comparison purposes.

Restaurant Selection Criteria

We focus on independent and local chain restaurants to get a representative sample of each city's poutine market. The following types of establishments are excluded:

  • National fast food chains (McDonald's, Burger King, Wendy's, A&W, etc.)
  • National poutine chains (Smoke's Poutinerie, New York Fries)
  • Quick-service chains (Harvey's, Tim Hortons, Dairy Queen)
  • Stadium, airport, or tourist-trap venues with inflated prices

Restaurants are discovered using Google Places API with the search query "poutine restaurant" near each city's coordinates. We aim for approximately 10-15 data points per city.

The Affordability Index

The affordability index measures how many minutes of minimum wage work are required to purchase a regular-sized classic poutine in each city. The formula is:

Affordability Index = (Regular Price / Provincial Minimum Wage) √ó 60

For example, if a regular poutine costs $12.00 and the provincial minimum wage is $15.00/hour:

($12.00 / $15.00) √ó 60 = 48 minutes

A higher affordability index means poutine is less affordable relative to local wages. This metric allows comparison between cities with different price levels and wage standards.

Minimum Wage Data

We use the general minimum wage for each province or territory (not special rates for specific industries or age groups). Minimum wage data is updated quarterly to reflect legislated changes.

Data Collection Process

  1. Restaurant Discovery: Google Places API identifies poutine-serving restaurants in each target city.
  2. Price Extraction: Restaurant websites and online menus are reviewed to find classic poutine prices. Prices are extracted manually with LLM assistance.
  3. Validation: Automated scripts check for outliers (prices more than 2 standard deviations from city mean), missing data, and stale information.
  4. Aggregation: City-level statistics (mean, median, min, max) are calculated and combined with minimum wage data.

Confidence Levels

Each price data point is assigned a confidence level:

  • High: Price clearly listed on official website or menu
  • Medium: Price inferred from menu (e.g., category pricing) or third-party source
  • Low: Estimated from similar items or older data; used only when necessary

Update Frequency

We aim to update price data quarterly. Minimum wage data is updated whenever provincial/territorial governments announce changes. The "last updated" date on the map reflects the most recent data processing run.

Limitations and Caveats

  • Sample size: Some smaller cities may have fewer data points, making averages less reliable.
  • Price volatility: Restaurant prices change frequently. Our data represents a snapshot in time.
  • Online menu availability: Not all restaurants have online menus, potentially biasing the sample toward establishments with web presence.
  • Regional variations: Within large metropolitan areas (e.g., Toronto, Montreal), prices may vary significantly by neighbourhood.
  • Taxes not included: Prices shown are menu prices before applicable taxes.
  • Delivery markups excluded: We use dine-in/pickup prices only.

Target Cities

Version 1 of the Poutine Index covers the top 20 Canadian Census Metropolitan Areas (CMAs) by population, plus four additional locations chosen for geographic coverage:

  • Banff, Alberta (tourist destination)
  • Yellowknife, Northwest Territories
  • Whitehorse, Yukon
  • Iqaluit, Nunavut
